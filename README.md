# Investing: an evidence-based starting point

## The answer in one sentence

For most people building wealth over decades, the strongest default is to clear expensive debt, hold an emergency fund, then invest automatically in a low-cost, tax-efficient, globally diversified portfolio of broad index funds whose stock/bond mix matches the date the money will be needed—and refuse to chase performance or time the market.

**Confidence: high** for the process; **unknown** for any individual's exact asset allocation until their finances, horizon, tax residence, and tolerance for loss are known.

This is a decision framework, not a prediction and not a list of fashionable tickers.

## Why this is the default

| Evidence | What it implies |
| --- | --- |
| William Sharpe's arithmetic shows that active investors collectively earn the market return before costs and less than the market after costs. | Start with low-cost market exposure. A higher-fee active strategy needs unusually strong evidence. |
| Hendrik Bessembinder found that the best-performing 4% of listed U.S. stocks explained the entire net gain of the U.S. stock market since 1926. | Concentrated stock picking creates a serious risk of missing the few extreme winners; broad diversification owns them automatically. |
| S&P Dow Jones Indices' SPIVA scorecards compare active funds with appropriate benchmarks and repeatedly document high long-horizon underperformance rates. | Do not mistake a manager's recent return for persistent skill. |
| FINRA investor guidance explains that asset allocation should reflect time horizon and risk tolerance, while diversification and rebalancing manage portfolio risk. | Allocation matters more than finding a supposedly perfect fund. |
| The SEC shows that ongoing fees reduce the capital that can compound, while FINRA reports that financial planners often recommend a liquid emergency fund of roughly three to six months of expenses. | Control costs and keep short-term cash needs out of volatile investments. |

The conclusion is not that index funds always win in every period. They do not. It is that low cost and broad diversification are controllable; future winners, manager skill, and market turning points are not reliably knowable in advance.

## The strategy

### 1. Build the financial base first

Use this order:

1. Keep enough cash for bills and foreseeable near-term expenses.
2. Capture any employer retirement-plan match, if available.
3. Repay high-interest debt. A guaranteed interest saving is a return without market risk.
4. Build an emergency reserve. Three to six months of essential expenses is a reasonable starting range; unstable income, dependants, or weak insurance justify more.
5. Invest money that will not be needed soon.

Do not put rent, emergency cash, a house deposit needed next year, or borrowed money into equities.

### 2. Set allocation from the deadline, not from recent returns

These are starting ranges, not universal prescriptions:

| When the money is needed | Starting point | Reason |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Under 3 years | 0–20% equities; the rest in high-quality short-duration cash or bonds matched to the spending currency | There may be too little time to recover from an equity crash. |
| 3–10 years | 30–70% equities; the rest in high-quality bonds/cash | Both growth and capital stability matter. |
| More than 10 years | 70–100% equities if a large temporary loss will not cause selling | A long horizon improves the capacity to wait, but does not remove risk. |

The correct equity percentage is the lower of:

- **risk capacity**: how much loss the plan can financially survive; and
- **risk tolerance**: how much loss the investor can endure without abandoning the plan.

If a 40–50% temporary equity loss would trigger panic selling, a 100% equity portfolio is not aggressive; it is badly designed.

### 3. Use a simple portfolio

A minimal implementation needs only two building blocks:

- a broad global equity index fund; and
- a broad, high-quality bond index fund or government securities appropriate to the investor's spending currency and horizon.

Select actual funds by checking:

- index coverage and diversification;
- total expense ratio and all trading/platform/custody costs;
- fund domicile, withholding tax, estate tax, and local tax treatment;
- liquidity, tracking difference, and fund size;
- whether bond currency risk is hedged to the currency of future spending; and
- whether the broker and fund are regulated and structurally appropriate in the investor's jurisdiction.

Fund domicile and taxes can dominate tiny fee differences. Do not copy a U.S.-specific portfolio blindly if you live elsewhere.

### 4. Automate behavior

- Invest a fixed amount after each payday.
- Direct new money toward underweight assets.
- Review once or twice per year.
- Rebalance when allocation materially departs from its target, preferably with contributions to reduce tax and trading costs.
- Change the plan only when goals, horizon, or financial circumstances change—not because markets became frightening or exciting.

Money already available for long-term investment has more expected time in the market if invested immediately. Phasing it in can still be rational when doing so prevents a behavioral mistake. That is risk management for the investor, not evidence that market timing works.

## What to avoid

- Individual stocks as the core portfolio.
- Leverage, options, CFDs, or margin before mastering loss mechanics.
- Crypto as a substitute for a diversified productive-asset portfolio.
- Funds selected from last year's leaderboard.
- High fees justified only by stories, forecasts, or backtests.
- Trading on news that is already public.
- An allocation that depends on selling before the next crash.

A small, explicitly capped speculation account is different from confusing speculation with the retirement plan.

## A worked example—not a recommendation

An investor with stable income, no expensive debt, a funded emergency reserve, and a retirement horizon beyond 20 years might choose 80% global equities and 20% high-quality bonds, automate monthly purchases, and rebalance annually. Someone saving for tuition in two years should hold mostly cash or short-duration government securities instead. The goal changes the answer.

The strategy should change only when better evidence or a clearly specified investor constraint changes the conclusion. Market forecasts alone are not evidence.
